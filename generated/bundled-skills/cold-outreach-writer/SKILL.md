# Cold Outreach Writer

Write cold outreach that gets opened, read, and replied to — by treating prospects like humans, not targets.

---

## The Core Truth

**Every cold message competes with 50 others.** The ones that work share three traits:
1. It's clearly about them, not you
2. There's one specific reason for reaching out
3. The ask is small (a reply, not a meeting)

---

## Step 1: Research Before Writing

Never write outreach without a personalization hook. Choose one:

```
TIER 1 — Deep personalization (highest reply rate):
  - Recent post or article they wrote
  - Company news (funding, launch, hiring, award)
  - Mutual connection reference
  - Specific problem visible in their role/company

TIER 2 — Role-specific personalization:
  - Pain point common to their function (VP Sales, Head of Engineering)
  - Industry-wide challenge they're likely facing
  - Tool or competitor they use (from LinkedIn, job postings)

TIER 3 — Generic ICP (lowest bar — use only at scale):
  - Company size / industry / growth signal
  - At minimum: get their name and company right
```

---

## Step 2: The Anatomy of a Cold Email

```
SUBJECT LINE — 3-6 words. Pattern interrupt or name/company reference.
OPENER — 1 sentence: the personalized hook. Shows you did homework.
BRIDGE — 1-2 sentences: why this is relevant to them specifically.
VALUE PROP — 1-2 sentences: what you do, who it helps, proof point.
CTA — 1 sentence: low-commitment ask. NOT "Can I schedule 30 minutes?"
SIGN-OFF — Name, title, company
P.S. (optional) — Second hook, social proof, or urgency
```

## Step 3: Email Templates by Type

### 3A. Trigger-Based (best performing)

Use when there's a specific event (funding, hire, launch, article).

```
Subject: Congrats on the [event] — quick thought

Hi [Name],

Saw [Company] just [raised Series A / launched X / hit Y milestone] — congrats.

[One sentence connecting their event to the problem you solve.]

We help [ICP] do [outcome] — [Company A] used us to [specific result] after a similar moment.

Worth a quick note back if [outcome] is a priority right now?

[Name]
```

### 3B. Pain-Led (most scalable)

Use when targeting a specific role with a known pain.

```
Subject: [Pain point] at [Company]?

Hi [Name],

Most [Job Title]s I talk to are dealing with [specific pain — be precise].

[Your solution] fixes this by [mechanism, not features].

[Customer] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe].

Is this on your radar?

[Name]
```

### 3C. Referral / Warm Intro

Use when there's a mutual connection.

```
Subject: [Mutual Name] suggested I reach out

Hi [Name],

[Mutual Name] thought we should connect — she mentioned [specific reason / context she gave].

[1 sentence: what you do and who it helps.]

Happy to share more if it's relevant — does [specific outcome] resonate with what you're focused on?

[Name]
```

### 3D. Content/Insight-Led

Use when prospect published something you can reference.

```
Subject: Your post on [topic]

Hi [Name],

Read your piece on [topic] — [one specific observation that shows you actually read it].

[Bridge to your product/service without being forced.]

Working on something in this space that [1 sentence on what you do]. Happy to share if useful.

[Name]
```

## Step 4: Multi-Touch Sequences

### 5-Touch Sequence (Standard B2B)

```
Day 1  — Email 1: Personalized hook + value prop + low-ask CTA
Day 3  — Email 2: Different angle. New proof point or pain framing.
Day 7  — Email 3: Content/insight share. No direct pitch.
Day 14 — Email 4: "Last try" framing. Direct + honest.
Day 21 — Email 5: Breakup email. Closes loop, sometimes generates replies.
```

### Sample Sequence Arc

```
Email 1: "Here's why I'm reaching out" (trigger or pain)
Email 2: "Here's proof it works" (case study or stat)
Email 3: "Here's something useful" (insight, tool, or content)
Email 4: "Direct ask" (I'll be blunt — is this a fit?)
Email 5: "Closing the loop" (I'll stop reaching out — feel free to reply if timing changes)
```

## Step 5: LinkedIn DM Rules

LinkedIn DMs are different from email — shorter, warmer, less formal.

```
RULE 1: Never pitch in the first message. Ever.
RULE 2: Start with a real observation or question.
RULE 3: Max 3 sentences in message 1.
RULE 4: Don't attach anything to the first message.
RULE 5: If they're a 1st connection — reference the connection.
```

**LinkedIn DM Template:**

```
Message 1 (connection request note or first message):
"[Name], saw your post on [topic] — [one genuine observation].
Would love to connect if you're open to it."

Message 2 (after they connect/reply):
"Thanks for connecting. Quick question — [relevant question tied to their role/company].
Asking because [brief why]."

Message 3 (pitch only if they engage):
"We help [ICP] with [outcome]. [Company] did [result].
Worth a quick call if this is on your radar?"
```

## Step 6: Subject Line Formulas

| Type | Formula | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Name/Company | "[First name] — quick thought" | "Sarah — quick thought" |
| Curiosity | "[Unexpected contrast or question]" | "Your pipeline vs. your quota" |
| Trigger | "Congrats on [event]" | "Congrats on the Series B" |
| Pain | "[Pain]?" | "Losing deals at demo stage?" |
| Referral | "[Name] suggested I reach out" | "Jake Chen suggested I reach out" |
| Number | "[#] [thing]" | "3 things [Company] is doing differently" |
| Pattern interrupt | Very short | "Quick thought" / "Intro" / "Hey" |

---

## Step 7: The Breakup Email (underrated)

Sent as the final touch. Often generates replies.

```
Subject: Closing the loop

Hi [Name],

I've sent a few notes and haven't heard back — completely understand, timing isn't always right.

I'll stop reaching out after this. If [pain point / outcome] ever becomes a priority, feel free to reply and I'll pick this back up.

Wishing you well either way.

[Name]
```

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Leading with "I" | Open with them, not you |
| Pitch in the first line | Hook first, ask last |
| "Can I get 30 minutes?" | "Worth a quick reply?" — lower friction |
| Long emails (5+ paragraphs) | Max 5 sentences for cold email |
| Vague value prop | Name a specific outcome with a number |
| Generic subject line | Use their name, company, or specific trigger |
| Sending the same message to everyone | One sentence of real personalization is enough |
| Following up daily | Follow sequence cadence — space it out |
